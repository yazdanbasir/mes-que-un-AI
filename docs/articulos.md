# Artículos — How to Use This Tab

## What it is

The Artículos tab pulls live Spanish football articles from six major publications — El País, Marca, AS, Sport, Mundo Deportivo, and La Vanguardia — and presents them as a reading feed. It refreshes automatically every 20 minutes in the background.

The tab is built around one idea: you learn Spanish by reading Spanish you mostly understand, on topics you already care about. Football is the domain. The app's job is to reduce the friction between you and the text.

---

## The reading flow

**Open an article you're actually curious about.** This matters. The affective filter — your brain's willingness to absorb — is higher when you genuinely want to know what happened. A match you watched last night, a transfer rumour you've been following, a player you track. Start there.

When you expand an article, two things appear:

1. **Lectura fácil** — a simplified 3–5 sentence Spanish story about the same event, generated in present tense with A2/B1 vocabulary. Read this first. It primes you with the who/what/result so the real article feels comprehensible rather than opaque. This is the TPRS scaffold: hear the simple version first, then encounter the real text already knowing the shape of the story.

2. **The full article** — the actual journalism. Read as far as you can. You don't need to understand every word. The target is roughly 90–95% comprehension — enough that meaning flows and the gaps get filled by context.

---

## Word and phrase lookup

Select any word or phrase you don't know. A tooltip appears with:

- A **Spanish definition** — written in Spanish, not English. This keeps you thinking in the language rather than code-switching. Even if you have to read the definition slowly, that processing is acquisition happening.
- An **English translation** — for when the definition alone isn't enough, or you want to confirm your reading.

A few ways to use this well:

- **Don't look up everything.** If you can infer meaning from context, let it pass. Looking up every unknown word breaks flow and trains dependence. Reserve it for words that genuinely block comprehension.
- **Look up phrases, not just words.** Spanish football writing is full of fixed expressions — *en propia puerta*, *fuera de juego*, *a falta de*. Select the whole phrase. The definition will make more sense than looking up each word alone.
- **Trust repeated exposure.** If a word keeps appearing across articles and you keep looking it up, that's the system working. You'll stop needing to look it up.

---

## Saving phrases

After a lookup, hit **Guardar** to save the phrase to your personal deck. What gets saved:

- The phrase itself
- The Spanish definition and English translation
- The full sentence it came from — the original context
- A grammar category tag (reflexive verb, subjunctive, idiom, football vocabulary, etc.)

The sentence matters. Words without context evaporate. Words tied to a moment — a Barça comeback, a Pedri injury update, a Lewandowski hat-trick — stick.

Saved phrases move into the **Revisión tab** where they get scheduled for spaced retrieval practice.

---

## Post-article comprehension check *(planned)*

After reading an article, a short conversational test based on what was shown — not the full article, just what the app extracted. Questions are in Spanish. Answers can be in English or Spanish. Ends with a production prompt: write one sentence in Spanish about what you just read. This is where passive comprehension becomes active output.

---

## Source filter

Use the filter button to read from a single publication. Different outlets have different styles:

- **El País** — broadsheet register, longer sentences, richer vocabulary. Good for pushing your level.
- **Marca / AS** — tabloid energy, short punchy sentences, lots of exclamation. Good for high-frequency football vocabulary with low cognitive load.
- **Sport / Mundo Dep.** — heavily Barça-focused, Catalan perspective. Repeat exposure to the same club vocabulary.

A useful session pattern: read Marca for a few quick articles to warm up (familiar vocabulary, fast reads), then switch to El País for the longer version of the same story.

---

## What you're actually doing

Every session in this tab is narrow reading — deliberately staying in one domain to maximise vocabulary recycling. The same words (*remate, despejar, fuera de juego, en propia puerta, partido intenso*) appear across every source, every week. That repetition-with-variation is what drives acquisition.

You don't need to study. You need to read enough, often enough, about something you care about.
